# Blood pressure glossary

## Systolic Blood Pressure

Systolic blood pressure (SBP) is one of the two values that make up a standard blood pressure reading, the other being diastolic blood pressure (DBP). It represents the higher of the two values and measures the pressure in the arteries when the heart contracts and pumps blood into the systemic circulation during a heartbeat. In other words, SBP is the maximum pressure exerted on the arterial walls by the forceful ejection of blood from the heart into the arteries.

A blood pressure reading is typically expressed as a pair of values, with SBP written over DBP, like "120/80 mm Hg," for example. In this reading, 120 mm Hg is the systolic blood pressure, and 80 mm Hg is the diastolic blood pressure.

Systolic blood pressure is particularly important because it reflects the pressure generated by the heart to propel blood into the arteries. It is influenced by factors such as cardiac output (the volume of blood pumped by the heart), the strength of the heart's contractions, and the overall condition of the cardiovascular system. Elevated systolic blood pressure is often associated with conditions like hypertension (high blood pressure), which can increase the risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, and other health issues.

Monitoring and managing systolic blood pressure, along with diastolic blood pressure, is essential for assessing and maintaining cardiovascular health and reducing the risk of related health problems.

## Diastolic Blood Pressure

Diastolic blood pressure (DBP) is one of the two values that make up a standard blood pressure reading, the other being systolic blood pressure (SBP). It represents the lower of the two values and measures the pressure in the arteries when the heart is at rest, specifically during the period between heartbeats when the heart is not actively contracting. This period is known as diastole.

A blood pressure reading is typically expressed as a pair of values, with DBP written under SBP, like "120/80 mm Hg," for example. In this reading, 120 mm Hg is the systolic blood pressure, and 80 mm Hg is the diastolic blood pressure.

Diastolic blood pressure reflects the pressure in the arteries when the heart is filling with blood and not actively pumping it out. It is an important component of a blood pressure measurement because it provides information about the resting pressure in the circulatory system. Elevated diastolic blood pressure can indicate increased peripheral resistance in the arteries, which can be associated with conditions like hypertension (high blood pressure).

Both systolic and diastolic blood pressure are significant for assessing cardiovascular health. Monitoring and managing these values are crucial in evaluating an individual's risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, and other related health problems. Blood pressure is typically expressed in millimeters of mercury (mm Hg) and is measured using a blood pressure cuff and a sphygmomanometer.

## Pulse Pressure

Pulse pressure (PP) is the numerical difference between the systolic blood pressure (SBP) and diastolic blood pressure (DBP). In other words, it is the result of subtracting the DBP from the SBP. Pulse pressure is a reflection of the force that the heart generates with each heartbeat and the elasticity of the arteries.

Mathematically, pulse pressure can be expressed as:

Pulse Pressure = SBP - DBP

Here's what each term means:

- SBP (Systolic Blood Pressure): This is the higher of the two blood pressure values, and it represents the pressure in the arteries when the heart contracts (systole) and pumps blood into the systemic circulation.

- DBP (Diastolic Blood Pressure): This is the lower of the two blood pressure values, and it represents the pressure in the arteries when the heart is at rest (diastole) between beats.

Pulse pressure can provide some insights into cardiovascular health. A wider pulse pressure may be an indicator of stiffer arteries, which can be seen in conditions like atherosclerosis. On the other hand, a very narrow pulse pressure can be a sign of various conditions, such as heart failure.

Typically, a healthy pulse pressure is around 40 mm Hg. However, it's important to note that pulse pressure varies between individuals, and what's considered normal can depend on a person's age, medical history, and other factors. While pulse pressure can offer some information about a person's cardiovascular status, it's not the sole indicator for diagnosis or evaluation. Healthcare professionals often consider pulse pressure alongside other clinical information and blood pressure values to make informed assessments of an individual's health.

## Mean Arterial Pressure

The mean arterial pressure (MAP) is a measure of the average pressure in a person's arteries during a single cardiac cycle. It is a critical physiological parameter because it reflects the perfusion pressure, or the pressure that drives blood flow through the systemic circulation, delivering oxygen and nutrients to the body's tissues.

MAP is typically calculated using the formula:

MAP = DBP + 1/3(SBP - DBP)

Where:

- DBP stands for Diastolic Blood Pressure, which is the lowest pressure in the arteries when the heart is at rest (between beats).
- SBP stands for Systolic Blood Pressure, which is the highest pressure in the arteries during each heartbeat.

The factor of 1/3 is used because, during the cardiac cycle, the diastolic phase (resting phase) is longer than the systolic phase (contracting phase), and the MAP reflects this relationship.

A normal range for MAP is usually around 70-100 mm Hg. Maintaining a sufficient MAP is important for ensuring adequate blood flow to the body's organs and tissues. Low MAP can lead to inadequate perfusion, which may result in organ damage, while high MAP can be associated with conditions like hypertension, which can strain the cardiovascular system. Healthcare professionals often monitor MAP to assess a patient's cardiovascular health and make clinical decisions accordingly.
